In the 1972 book and four-part television series, Ways of Seeing, John Berger describes the relationship between what we see and what we know as “never settled”. Using the sunset as an example, the author writes / says: “each evening we see the sun set. We know that the earth is turning away from it. Yet the knowledge, the explanation, never quite fits the sight.”

Today, in 2020, images are often consumed through digital interfaces. The windows through which images are presented to us intensify the already perplexing and unsettling mystery of what is real and fake, and what is true and false. Our digital footprints are tracked, traded, and parsed through sophisticated algorithms to encourage and perpetuate consumerism. But these algorithms were / are never sophisticated enough to filter lies among a sea of disorienting images and messages, sent by anyone from anywhere; nor were they ever designed to be objective enough to do so. The openness of the web is a double-edged sword. Anyone can participate and contribute by sharing, linking, tagging, etc... But images, among other forms of information, can be and are often shared, linked, and tagged devoid of context, leading to misinformation. It might be because of this overwhelming anxiety of having to constantly question the realness and truthfulness of what I see, that I find so much solace in the image of a missing / broken image.

When the source of an image cannot be located, some web browsers render a placeholder icon to indicate that an image was or will be shown, but is for now, “missing” or “broken”. Sometimes it is accompanied by a textual alternative (a.k.a. alt-text) that attempts to describe what the image would have depicted, usually written by the same person who put the image there.

A missing / broken image often presents itself as a mystery and / or an injury. It usually looks like a pixelated veil with a question mark or an “X”, or a fracture that implies a preceding accident. A missing / broken image is rarely the reason I stay on a page, but it is often the reason I pause scrolling, even for a brief second. A missing / broken image is relatively small, but always significant. The way it carries itself so quietly in a reserved manner among a sea of noisy pixels attracts my attention. A missing / broken image is a shy gesture and a seductive interlude.

What is in a missing / broken image, I wonder? Could it be more than just the result of a technical error, a human mistake, an outdated or relocated file address?

Could it be a mirror? A mirror that reflects an image of myself? Because I almost always sense a slight disappointment arising within me when I encounter this image. I think it reveals just how much I crave to be fed with content from the feed, that even a brief intermission can be agitating. Perhaps what is hidden underneath the mysterious veil of a missing image is my acquired and questionable taste of modernity. Which is characterized by speed, efficiency, a desire to always stay connected, and the impatience and intolerance towards any sign of slowness or weakness: the slowness of an internet connection or the weakness of a wifi signal.

Could it be a neutralizing agent in the era of post-truth social media, where biased beliefs are portrayed, framed, and oftentimes forced to be accepted as truths? Could unseeing be greater, better, and healthier than seeing? Could the missing / broken image be the browser’s built-in defense and survival mechanism, a self-censoring system designed to help us counter anxiety?

Could it be someone’s secret? Your secret? My secret? For it is so mysterious, shy, and easily overlooked, could it be a strategy for communication that effectively bypasses security checks and the scanning eye? An apparatus that carries messages which would otherwise be censored, blocked, or remain undelivered, unseen, and unread?

Is the lack of content the content itself? A missing / broken image IS an image, afterall. Is a missing image really missing, though? Should we look for it? Should we post flyers and reward whoever finds it? Or is it just as present, if not more present than all the other images? Is a broken image really broken, though? Like a broken heart or a fractured bone, looking to be amended, to be pieced back together? Will it really no longer serve a meaningful function if it stays fractured...?

What is in a missing / broken image, anyway?
